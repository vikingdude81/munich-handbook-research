Because Actaeon the hunter has seen the 
proud virgin Diana naked while bathing, 
she turns him into a deer, and he is then 
torn to pieces by his own bloodhounds. 
Giordano Bruno interprets this legend in 
his last work Ofthe heroic passions (1585) 
as a parable ofthe drama ofthe process of 
knowledge. 
"Here Actaeon represents the intellect, on 
the hunt for divine wisdom at the moment 
of grasping divine beauty." Just when 
he thinks he grasps Sophia in the glass of 
outer nature, and lifts the veil from her 
lunar mystery, he himself becomes the 
victim or object of his own striving. "He 
saw himself transformed into that which 
he sought, and realized that he himself 
had become a much-desired prey for his 
hounds, his thoughts. Because he had 
actually drawn the godhead into himself, 
it was no longer necessary to seek them 
outside himself." 
Actaeon is Bruno's new heroic man who, 
killed by his many large hounds, is radic- 
ally inverted. "Here, his life in the mad, 
sensuous, blind and fantastic world comes 
to an end, and from now on he leads a 
spiritual life. He lives the life of the gods." 
Titian, Diana and Actaeon, 1559
